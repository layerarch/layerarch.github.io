## Typical Architectural Process for Residential Additions

Every project is unique, but there is a typical workflow that seems to fit with most projects. Sometimes clients may approach us starting from step 1, whereas others may have already done some of the leg work on their own, either way works. 

### 1. Land Survey

Survey – One of the first things you should do when setting out is acquire a legal land survey prepared by an Ontario Licensed Surveyor. The survey will provide you with critical information such as: 
* Lot boundaries
* Lot areas
* Current building setbacks for your property and your neighbours
* Topography (usually an additional fee but necessary when adding upwards)
* Tree location and trunk diameters

It is also important to ask for the survey in both .PDF and .DWG format. The PDF allows for printing and viewing in a PDF viewer, whereas the .DWG file will allow your architect to bring the digital file into their drafting software without having to manually input the information. 

There is a chance you may already have a survey from when you purchased the home. Generally it does not matter how old a survey is, as long as it was prepared by a licensed surveyor and the information within the survey is legible. 

Another option is to reach out to the City for a Routine Disclosure to determine whether they have one on file from a previous owners renovation project, or from the initial construction. Your architect can also file this application on your behalf, but the City timeline for responding to Routine Disclosures can be anywhere from a few weeks to several months. If you can't afford to wait that long hiring a surveyor to prepare a new one is generally the way to go. 

### 2. Measured Drawings

An equally critical first step is to document your existing house through a set of measured drawings. This process entails your architect going to site to take measurements of your house. They will typically need to measure the entire outside perimeter, interior dimensions of every room, locations and sizes of doors, windows, plumbing, electrical, and mechanical equipment. 

This process typically utilises 3D scanners, laser measurers, or just a measuring tape, paper, and pencil. Once all the information is gathered your architect will convert the raw information into a set of floor plans showing the existing layout and dimensions.

It is also not uncommon for your architect to require information on what is inside your existing walls. Information generally needed includes: 
* Composition and thickness of the walls components
* Size of structural members
* Insulation values
* Presence of vapour barrier and other water protection membranes
* Check for condition of wall due to known damages or defects

Sometimes, but not always, gathering this critical information requires making exploratory openings made by cutting through the finish materials to examine the inner workings of the wall. 

Where permitted you can always try to gather this information in non-destructive ways. Such as, by temporarily removing potlights, bathroom fans or by accessing  unfinished areas of the house such as a crawlspace or attic.

If destructive openings are a must, and your renovation is several months to years down the road, your architect can assist with retaining a contractor to patch up any openings made. 

### 3. Schematic Design

The schematic design phase is the initial architectural step in the design process. With all of the existing lot and building information available, this phase entails creating a series of sketches, concept layouts, and 3D models to explore and define the design of your addition.

This is also when your architect will want to confirm zoning requirements applicable to your property. Key zoning considerations it Toronto typically include:
* Required setbacks from property lines
* Maximum building height
* Lot coverage limits
* Angular plane requirements
* Permissions for additional dwelling units

The culmination of this phase results in a cleaned up digital set of floor plan, elevation, and section drawings to fully showcase the layout and 3D massing for the design.

### 4. Zoning Submission (Zoning Applicable Law Certificate)

At some point in the processes you will have likely had a conversation with your architect about whether your design will be permitted as-of-right, meaning the proposed addition does not contravene the Zoning By-law in any way, or whether you will require a Minor Variance to be approved through the Committee of Adjustment. 

If applying as of right your architect simply needs to update the  with a site plan and include the relevant site statistics, then update the schematic design drawing package with key zoning information, dimensions, and labels. This update drawing package, along with a filled out application and the previously acquired survey survey will be use to make the Zoning Submission.

If you want to build beyond what is permitted as of right by the Zoning By-law, then an application to the Committee of Adjustments will eventually be required. You can submit straight CoA and skip the initial Zoning Submission, however, it is strongly recommended to make the Zoning application first to ensure all deviations from the Zoning By-law are flagged by City staff. 

For minor deviations from the zoning By-law the ZC set already prepared should suffice. For larger deviations, special presentation materials to better present your design and the deviations from the By-law will assist during your hearing. 
You or your architect will also need to appear before the Committee during a public meeting to explain your project and need for the Minor Variance.  

Understanding Toronto zoning bylaws early allows the design to respond to real constraints rather than idealized assumptions.




The City charges a flat fee for these types of applications which are posted online, which was $2,228.98 at the time of writing this post in 2026. 



Recent provincial changes now allow for more generous building envelopes when a secondary suite, such as a basement apartment, is included in the proposal. In many situations, this can enable significantly larger additions — but only when coordinated properly during the permit process.
